My Background

My job experience and educational background.

University of Cincinnati

I am a Computer Engineering student at the University of Cincinnati. I was drawn to the campus as I had been visiting the campus since I was a kid, and I started in Aerospace Engineering. As I began to progress through the program, I realized I had a passion for the software and hardware projects I was participating in during the first engineering classes.

This let to me tranfering to Computer Engineering. This was early enough of a change that I did not lose much progress, and I was taking classes I was directly interested in, especially in my second and third year. Courses such as Network Analysis and Electronics I expanded on the circuit analysis I learned during my time in Aviation Maintenance.

Here I also gained exposure to software and firmware development, where embedded programming skills were developed through courses like Intro to EECE, where I began my journey in C++ programming, my strongest language to date and gave me the hardware exposure that I was striving for.

Target

Target was my second job, coming out of high school I needed something to fund my college experience in the Cincinnati area. I settled in at Target during my first semester at UC. While "summer jobs" like this are often not considered to be fundamental in a student's development, I found this to be very important to how I act professionally.

Here I was surrounded by both coworkers and customers that have experienced many types of work and education. My manager during a significant part of my time here was a fundamental part of my development, pushing me to try more and find new ways to work.

Miami Valley Career Technology Center

My time at MVCTC studying Aviation Maintenance was my first real dive into complex, hands-on technical work. While my career path eventually shifted toward computer engineering, the foundation I built here is something I use every day.

Learning how to troubleshoot and maintain aircraft systems taught me a level of practical problem-solving that you simply cannot get from a textbook. It is also where I was first exposed to circuit analysis, which gave me a massive head start when I began taking electronics courses at UC.

More importantly, aviation demands strict attention to detail and a deep understanding of how individual components affect an entire system. I still apply that exact same rigorous mindset today, whether I am routing traces on a custom PCB or debugging firmware.

The Rest

Outside of formal education and work, my background is mostly defined by a persistent urge to build, fix, and understand how things operate at a fundamental level. I am a strong believer in the "Buy It For Life" philosophy, which naturally pushed me toward designing and repairing my own hardware.

Whether I am maintaining my car, iterating on PCB designs, or fine-tuning a custom Linux desktop environment, I treat my hobbies as an extension of my engineering education. I don't just want to write code that lives on a screen; I want to create tangible, durable systems from the ground up.
